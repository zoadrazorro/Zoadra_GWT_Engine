Even Kant’s transcendental framework, foundational for Schopenhauer’s critique, often relegated life to the *phenomenal* realm of temporal succession, neglecting its constitutive role in consciousness itself. This part fundamentally revises that paradigm: *De Vita* establishes life not as a biological condition or moral outcome, but as the very mode of Being wherein consciousness is embodied within the irreducible flow of time.

This reorientation is not merely theoretical; it addresses a profound dislocation in modern thought. Contemporary discourse, as James observed in *The Principles of Psychology*, often fragments consciousness into isolated sensations or abstracted cognitive functions, neglecting its *temporal totality*. By contrast, *De Vita* synthesizes Schopenhauer’s conception of the *will*—not as a blind force, but as the dynamic, embodied *drive* of conscious being—as the substratum of life’s temporal unfolding. This aligns with Kant’s insistence that time is the *a priori* form of inner sense , yet transcends Kant by demonstrating how this temporal structure *constitutes* consciousness rather than merely organizing it. Nietzsche’s *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* further informs this synthesis: the will to power is not a mere striving for dominance, but the creative, temporal *embodiment* of consciousness—exemplified in Zarathustra’s call to embrace the “eternal return” as the full *lived* expression of existence.

*De Vita* will establish three interdependent theses: First, life is the *embodiment* of consciousness in temporal process, where the body is not a vessel for the mind, but the very medium through which temporal being manifests. Second, this embodiment renders life inherently *creative* and *moral*—not through external law, but through the will’s self-actualizing temporal movement, as Schopenhauer’s critique of Kantian ethics reveals. Third, the modern crisis of meaning stems precisely from the disconnection between consciousness and this embodied temporal process, a fragmentation James lamented as the “inexhaustible” distraction of the “crowded age.” By grounding creativity and morality in the Luminous Field, *Metaluminous Ethica* avoids nihilism.
